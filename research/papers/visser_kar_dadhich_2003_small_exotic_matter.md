# Traversable Wormholes with Arbitrarily Small Energy Condition Violations

**Authors:** Matt Visser (Victoria University of Wellington), Sayan Kar (IIT Kharagpur), Naresh Dadhich (IUCAA, Pune)  
**Year:** 2003  
**Published:** Physical Review Letters, 90, 201102 (2003)  
**arXiv:** gr-qc/0301003  
**DOI:** 10.1103/PhysRevLett.90.201102

---

## Abstract
Demonstrates that traversable wormholes require violations of the averaged null energy condition (exotic matter), but that the *amount* of exotic matter needed can be made arbitrarily small. Introduces a "volume integral quantifier" to measure how much NEC violation a wormhole requires.

---

## Background

Morris & Thorne (1988) showed traversable wormholes require exotic matter but gave no precise measure of how much. Subsequent work (especially Ford & Roman quantum inequalities) made it seem like large amounts were needed. This paper shows the situation is subtler.

---

## The Volume Integral Quantifier

Define the "total amount of exotic matter" threading the wormhole throat as:

```
Ω = ∮ [ρ + p_r] dV
```

Where:
- `ρ` = energy density
- `p_r` = radial pressure (along the throat axis)
- `ρ + p_r` < 0 is the Null Energy Condition (NEC) violation condition
- The integral is over the entire wormhole geometry

**Key result:** For a general class of traversable wormhole geometries, Ω can be made arbitrarily close to zero (Ω → 0⁻) while the wormhole remains traversable.

---

## How Arbitrarily Small Exotic Matter Is Achieved

The trick is geometric: make the wormhole throat extremely thin (small cross-section) while keeping a large traversable region. As the throat cross-section → 0, the total integrated NEC violation → 0.

**The catch:** As the throat shrinks, it becomes geometrically incompatible with human-scale traversal. A wormhole with Ω → 0 has a throat that is effectively a mathematical surface with no interior volume — you cannot physically pass through it.

The paper explicitly states: "the amount of exotic matter can be made arbitrarily small" but this comes at the cost of a "thin-shell" geometry that is:
1. Not traversable by any macroscopic object
2. Extremely sensitive to perturbations
3. Not the human-traversable portal of science fiction

---

## Implications for Engineering

| Parameter | Large wormhole (human traversable) | Minimized exotic matter wormhole |
|-----------|------------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Throat radius | ~1 m (for human passage) | → 0 |
| Exotic matter Ω | ~−Jupiter mass | → 0 |
| Traversable by humans? | Yes (in theory) | No — throat is microscopically thin |
| Stability | Requires sustained exotic matter support | Extremely fragile |

**Summary:** The paper proves a mathematical result (Ω can be minimized) but does not provide a path to engineering a human-traversable wormhole with small exotic matter. The two requirements (large throat + small exotic matter) are in tension — you can have one or the other, not both.

---

## Assessment

**Important paper:** Clarifies a key question in wormhole physics — the amount of exotic matter is not fixed by topology, it depends on geometry. This is a genuine physics insight.

**Does not help with engineering:** The "arbitrarily small" exotic matter result applies only to geometries with vanishingly small throats, not to the large-throat traversable wormholes we'd need for teleportation.

**Status:** Established theoretical physics. Peer-reviewed, widely cited.
